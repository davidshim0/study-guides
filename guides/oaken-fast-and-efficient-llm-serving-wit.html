Squeezing billions of cached tokens into tight memory

When you batch 256 LLM requests, the KV cache explodes. Traditional quantization either kills accuracy or never translates into real speedup. Oaken does both—an online-offline hybrid that cuts bitwidth 70% while the GPU stays fast.

1Why batching LLM inference is memory-bound▾

Batching multiple requests boosts throughput when operands are shared (e.g., weights in feed-forward layers). But attention layers have un-shareable, per-request KV cache — no on-chip reuse, pure memory bandwidth bottleneck. For large batches at long sequences, the KV cache grows to hundreds of MB or GB, all streamed from DRAM every decode step.

Now you're ready: Oaken's goal is to shrink that per-request KV footprint so the same bandwidth serves more requests, faster.
2The bandwidth-capacity tradeoff in memory▾

HBM delivers 2 TB/s but only 80 GB. LPDDR offers 256 GB but 1.1 TB/s. For small models & batches, bandwidth wins (HBM). For large models & batches, you run out of HBM capacity and saturate. You can't have both — unless you compress what you store.

Now you're ready: Oaken makes LPDDR's 1.1 TB/s feel like 2 TB/s by reading 70% fewer bits, unlocking the capacity advantage.
3Why existing KV quantization is slow▾

Methods like KVQuant identify outlier channels via a topK sort every token — O(n log n) overhead that negates the compression gains. Others use mixed precision (FP16 outliers, INT4 inliers), which requires custom kernels and complicates memory layout. The result: big accuracy, but tiny speedup.

Now you're ready: Oaken avoids online sorting by profiling thresholds offline once, then applying them online in O(n) per-token.
4Outlier thresholds are model-specific, not data-specific▾

The paper's key empirical finding: KV value ranges vary across models and layers, but remain consistent across datasets profiled in the paper (Wikitext2, PIQA, Hellaswag). So you can profile on Wikitext2, extract four thresholds per layer, and reuse them on the evaluated datasets — no per-request calibration.

Now you're ready: Oaken's offline profiling (~100 inferences, ~10 min for Llama2-70B) is a one-time cost that applies to the tested workloads.
5Three quantization groups: outer, middle, inner▾

Oaken splits each KV vector into three groups based on magnitude: outer (large outliers), middle (inliers, ~90%), inner (near-zero outliers). Four thresholds define the boundaries. The middle group is quantized to 4 bits; outer & inner to 5 bits after group-shift.

Now you're ready: The group-shift trick (subtract threshold before quantizing) narrows the range so outliers can fit in 5 bits instead of requiring FP16.
6Dense-and-sparse encoding saves storage▾

Inliers (middle group, 4 bits) are stored densely. Outliers (outer & inner, 5 bits each) are stored in COO sparse format — normally 6 index bits + 1 group bit + 5 value bits = 12 bits per entry. Oaken's trick: fuse 4 of the 5 value bits into the zeroed dense slots, leaving only 8 bits in the sparse table (6 index + 1 group + 1 sign).

Now you're ready: The fused scheme is memory-aligned (8-bit entries) and cuts outlier storage from 23 bits to 8 bits.
7Quantization / dequantization engines in DMA▾

Oaken integrates custom engines into the accelerator's DMA unit. The quantization engine (on write) splits values into three groups, applies group-shift, quantizes to 4/5 bits, and builds dense + sparse matrices. The dequantization engine (on read) reverses the process in streaming fashion — no full-cache buffering.

Now you're ready: Table 4 reports quantization engine area at 1.86% and dequantization engine at 6.35% of compute-core area, summing to 8.21%. The MMU area is reported separately. These engines hide their latency by overlapping with memory access.
8Page-based MMU for dense & sparse matrices▾

The MMU maintains two tables: one for dense pages (predictable size), one for sparse pages (variable size). Both map virtual addresses to physical pages and track transfer sizes. This design enables burst reads (sequential KV tokens across pages) and avoids fragmentation — maximal bandwidth utilization.

Now you're ready: The MMU ensures that quantization's complexity doesn't fragment memory or kill burst efficiency.
The whole story in one place

Oaken in a nutshell

The problem, the hybrid solution, and the hardware that makes it fast.

When you batch LLM inference, the KV cache size explodes — proportional to batch size × sequence length. On an A100 GPU with HBM, large batches run out of capacity (80 GB). The paper's baseline setup uses two A100 GPUs with pipeline parallelism (160 GB capacity) for larger models. On an LPDDR-based accelerator, large batches saturate bandwidth (1.1 TB/s). Quantization could help, but existing methods either (1) lose too much accuracy (channel-reordered coarse quantization), or (2) stay accurate but impose prohibitive runtime overhead (online topK sorting or mixed-precision kernels).

Oaken's fix: an online-offline hybrid KV cache quantization. Offline, it profiles ~100 inferences to extract four magnitude thresholds per layer — data-agnostic across the tested datasets but model-specific. Online, it splits each new KV vector into three groups (outer, middle, inner) using those thresholds, applies group-shift (a reversible range transformation) to narrow the range before quantizing wide-magnitude outliers into 5 bits, and stores inliers densely (4 bits) while fusing outliers into the dense matrix's zeroed slots plus a sparse COO table.

Hardware-wise, Oaken integrates a quantization engine (on KV write) and dequantization engine (on KV read) into the accelerator's DMA unit, plus a page-based MMU that handles dense + sparse matrices in burst mode. Table 4 reports the quantization engine at 1.86% and dequantization engine at 6.35% of compute-core area, totaling 8.21%. The engines hide latency by overlapping with memory ops — the MMU ensures no fragmentation or bandwidth loss.

The payoff: Llama2-13B at batch=256 sees 1.58× throughput vs A100 + QServe, up to 70% bitwidth reduction, and only 0.87% average accuracy loss vs FP16. The sweet spot is LPDDR capacity + Oaken compression ≈ HBM bandwidth, but with 3× the capacity. Results vary by model and configuration; the crossover depends on memory pressure and workload characteristics.

🍯 Key takeaways

  • Batched LLM inference is memory-bound — the KV cache scales with batch×sequence and can't be shared across requests.
  • HBM vs LPDDR: bandwidth vs capacity. Oaken breaks the tradeoff by making LPDDR's 1.1 TB/s act like 2 TB/s via compression.
  • Offline profiling extracts four thresholds per layer (consistent across tested datasets); online quantization applies them in O(n) per token.
  • Three groups: outer (5-bit), middle (4-bit), inner (5-bit). Group-shift is a reversible transformation that narrows ranges before quantization.
  • Fused dense-and-sparse: inliers dense, 4 bits of outliers embedded in zeroed slots, 8-bit sparse COO for the rest.
  • Custom engines in DMA + page-based MMU enable burst reads with 8.21% area overhead (quant+dequant) and hidden latency.
  • Result: up to 1.58× throughput, 70% bitwidth cut, 0.87% avg accuracy loss.

How to think about Oaken

Each picture stands alone. Every card ends with an honest note about where it stops being true.

Offline profiling · One-time calibration

Tuning a guitar once

You tune your guitar's strings to standard pitch once, then play thousands of songs without retuning. Oaken profiles KV distribution on ~100 inferences to find outlier thresholds per layer, then applies those thresholds to all future requests — one-time 10-min cost, lifetime benefit.

Where it breaks: If the model changes (fine-tuned), you re-profile. A guitar's strings also need retuning after string changes or temperature shifts.
Three groups · Magnitude-based splitting

Three filing cabinets by size

You have three cabinets: one for large items (rare), one for normal stuff (90%), one for tiny screws (also rare). Oaken splits each KV vector into outer (4%, large), middle (90%, normal), inner (6%, near-zero). The middle group fits in a dense 4-bit drawer; the outer/inner need 5-bit handling but are sparse.

Where it breaks: Real filing cabinets don't share space. Oaken's fused encoding embeds 4 bits of outliers into the dense matrix's zeroed slots — a trick cabinets can't do.
Group-shift · Compress wide ranges

Resetting an altimeter to sea level

An altimeter reading 5,000 ft needs 13 bits to represent. Subtract the local ground elevation (5,000) and it reads 0 ft, needing only a few bits for elevation changes. Oaken's group-shift subtracts the threshold (T_o_hi or T_o_lo) from outliers before quantizing, converting a wide range into a narrow one that fits 5 bits. At dequantization, add back the threshold — it's reversible.

Where it breaks: Altimeters are reset manually; Oaken's shift is automatic per-group. Also, the analogy implies a single baseline; Oaken uses four thresholds (two pairs for outer/inner). The shift plus subsequent quantization is lossy overall, though the shift itself is reversible.
Fused encoding · Embedding into zeroed slots

Packing jewelry into a suitcase's gaps

Your suitcase is mostly clothes (dense), but you tuck small jewelry into the empty corners where folded shirts leave gaps. Oaken stores inliers densely (4 bits), then embeds 4 bits of each outlier value into the zeroed elements of the dense matrix — the rest (6 index + 1 group + 1 sign) goes into a sparse table.

Where it breaks: A suitcase's gaps are random; Oaken's zeroed slots are systematically placed (wherever outliers were extracted). Also, you can't re-embed the same jewelry twice — Oaken's scheme is deterministic.
Hardware integration · DMA engines

A dishwasher with built-in drying

You don't hand-dry dishes after the wash cycle; the dishwasher's drying element does it automatically in the same machine. Oaken's quantization and dequantization engines live inside the DMA unit — KV values are quantized on write and dequantized on read, seamlessly, with no external controller needed.

Where it breaks: A dishwasher's drying is serial (wash → dry). Oaken overlaps quantization with memory writes and dequantization with memory reads — it's more like a pipelined assembly line.
MMU design · Two tables, one address space

Dual library catalog (hardcover vs paperback)

A library has two catalogs: one for hardcovers (uniform size, predictable shelving) and one for rare manuscripts (variable size, special storage). Both share the same checkout system. Oaken's MMU maintains two page tables (dense, sparse) but operates in a single address space, enabling burst reads and avoiding fragmentation.

Where it breaks: A library's catalogs are separate physical systems. Oaken's MMU is one hardware unit with two logical tables — the analogy undersells the integration.
Sweet spot · Offline-online hybrid

Pre-sorting mail at the post office

The post office pre-sorts mail by zip code once (offline), then carriers deliver using those zones every day (online, fast). Alternatives: sort every delivery (slow) or deliver to random addresses (fast, wrong). Oaken pre-profiles thresholds (offline), then applies them per-token (online, O(n)) — avoiding both slow sorting and inaccurate coarse quantization.

Where it breaks: Mail zones change rarely; Oaken's thresholds never change after profiling (unless you fine-tune the model). Also, post offices sort mail in real-time too; the analogy oversimplifies the offline-online split.
The full explanation

How it actually works

Section by section, with the paper's key diagrams rebuilt as visuals.

1 · The problem — batching hits the memory wall

LLM serving systems batch requests to boost throughput. Feed-forward layers benefit: weights are shared across all requests, enabling on-chip reuse and compute-bound execution. But attention layers are different — the KV cache is per-request and un-shareable. For large batches at long sequences, the KV cache grows to hundreds of MB or GB. Every decode step reads the entire KV history from DRAM — pure memory bandwidth bottleneck. GPU underutilization is 60% during generation (Fig. 3c), and throughput saturates as batch grows (Fig. 5b).

🌱
Concretely: The paper's GPU baseline uses two A100 GPUs with pipeline parallelism, providing 160 GB total HBM capacity. At large batches, models like Llama2-70B run into memory pressure. An LPDDR accelerator has 256 GB capacity but only 1.1 TB/s — throughput saturates at large batches due to bandwidth. You can't have both.

2 · Why existing quantization is slow

Recent KV cache quantization methods fall into two camps:

ApproachTechniqueLimitationExample
High-accuracy, slowPer-vector topK sorting or mixed precision (FP16 outliers)O(n log n) overhead or custom kernels kill speedupKVQuant, KIVI
Fast, low-accuracyChannel reordering + coarse per-group quantizationIgnores within-group variance → large accuracy lossQServe, Atom, Tender
⚠
The gap: KVQuant achieves lower accuracy loss than Oaken (0.87% Oaken vs ~0.33% KVQuant on average, implying Oaken is 0.54% worse) but requires sorting 4096-element vectors every token — hardware can't hide that cost. QServe is fast but loses more accuracy. The sweet spot is missing.

3 · Observation — KV distribution is model-specific, not data-specific

The paper's key empirical finding (Fig. 6): the min-max range of KV values varies across models and layers but remains consistent across the datasets tested (Wikitext2, PIQA, Hellaswag). This means you can profile on one dataset, extract thresholds, and reuse them on the evaluated datasets — no per-request calibration needed.

Moreover, while high-magnitude values cluster in specific channels (as prior work observed), there are exceptions to this pattern — discontinuous outliers that appear in otherwise-typical channels (Fig. 6c). This motivates Oaken's per-vector grouping (not per-channel), with multiple quantization groups within each vector.

4 · Offline profiling — extract four thresholds per layer

Oaken runs ~100 inferences on a sample dataset (e.g., Wikitext2) and collects topK values at each layer. For each KV vector, it identifies four thresholds: T_o_lo, T_i_lo, T_i_hi, T_o_hi, corresponding to the boundaries of three groups:

  • Outer group (4%): x < T_o_lo or x > T_o_hi (large outliers)
  • Middle group (90%): T_o_lo ≤ x < T_i_lo or T_i_hi < x ≤ T_o_hi (inliers)
  • Inner group (6%): T_i_lo ≤ x ≤ T_i_hi (near-zero outliers)

These thresholds are averaged across the 100 runs and stored per-layer. Profiling Llama2-70B takes ~10 minutes — a one-time cost. The thresholds are model-specific and apply to the tested workloads.

5 · Online quantization — apply thresholds in O(n)

At inference time, for each newly-generated KV token (one vector per attention head), Oaken:

  1. Splits the vector into three groups using the precomputed thresholds — O(n) scan, no sorting.
  2. Applies group-shift: For the outer group, subtract T_o_hi from values > T_o_hi and T_o_lo from values < T_o_lo. For the middle group, subtract T_i_hi or T_i_lo as appropriate. This narrows the range before quantization.
  3. Computes scaling factors: For each group, find min/max and compute σ = (2^m - 1) / (max - min), where m is the target bitwidth (4 for middle, 5 for outer/inner).
  4. Quantizes: Q(x) = round((x - min) × σ). Middle group → 4 bits, outer/inner → 5 bits. The overall process (shift + quantization) is lossy.
🍯
Key difference: Oaken computes the scaling factor online (per-token min/max) but determines which values belong to which group offline. This avoids the O(n log n) sort while still adapting to per-token variance.

6 · Fused dense-and-sparse encoding

Oaken stores the quantized KV cache using a hybrid layout:

  • Dense matrix: 4-bit entries for the middle group (inliers), ~90% of values. At positions corresponding to outliers, the dense matrix holds 4 bits of the outlier's quantized value (the lower 4 of 5 bits).
  • Sparse COO table: For each outlier, store 6 bits (index), 1 bit (group: outer vs inner), 1 bit (sign) = 8 bits per entry. The index points to the dense matrix location where the other 4 bits are embedded.

Normally, storing a 5-bit outlier in sparse format would require 5 value + 6 index + 1 group = 12 bits per entry, misaligned. Oaken's fusion reduces it to 8 bits in the sparse table (memory-aligned) plus the 4 bits already present in the dense matrix — a net 12 bits total but with better layout and no padding waste.

7 · Quantization engine (on KV write)

The quantization engine sits in the DMA unit (Fig. 9a). Incoming FP16 KV values are:

  1. Decomposed into three groups based on offline thresholds, with group-shift applied.
  2. Quantized separately: inliers to 4 bits, outliers to 5 bits.
  3. Merged into a dense matrix (OR gate combines inliers + embedded outlier bits).
  4. Sparse table generated via a zero-remove shifter that compacts outlier indices.

The engine is pipelined and overlaps with memory writes — latency is hidden.

8 · Dequantization engine (on KV read)

The dequantization engine (Fig. 9b) reverses the process in streaming fashion:

  1. Inlier dequantizer buffers dense data.
  2. Outlier dequantizer reads sparse COO entries, performs a zero-insert to restore positions, and merges the 4 embedded bits from the dense matrix with the 1 sign bit from the sparse table to reconstruct 5-bit outliers.
  3. Both streams merged via OR gate and forwarded to the processing unit.

No full-cache buffering is needed — the engine operates token-by-token in a streaming manner, hiding latency by overlapping with memory reads.

9 · Memory management unit (MMU) — page-based dual tables

The MMU (Fig. 10) maintains two management tables:

  • Dense table: Predictable page sizes (4-bit entries), virtual-to-physical mappings updated on KV write.
  • Sparse table: Variable page sizes (8-bit entries per outlier), dynamically allocated.

Both tables share a single address space at page granularity. When reading KV cache, the MMU issues burst reads for sequential tokens (dense pages are laid out contiguously by design), maximizing bandwidth utilization. The page-based approach avoids fragmentation and burst order issues — physical bandwidth approaches the 1.1 TB/s limit even with the fused layout.

Follow the numbers, step by step

Worked examples

Four traces from the paper, computed exactly.

① Offline profiling — extracting thresholds for Llama2-7B layer 6

Run 100 inferences on Wikitext2. For layer 6 keys, the topK values (4%, 10%, 90%, 96% percentiles) average to: T_o_lo = -8.2, T_i_lo = -2.1, T_i_hi = 2.1, T_o_hi = 8.2. What do these mean?

  1. Outer group (4%): Values < -8.2 or > 8.2. These are large-magnitude outliers.
  2. Middle group (90%): Values in [-8.2, -2.1) or (2.1, 8.2]. These are inliers.
  3. Inner group (6%): Values in [-2.1, 2.1]. These are near-zero outliers.
  4. These thresholds are stored and reused for all future inferences on Llama2-7B layer 6 with the tested datasets, regardless of specific input.
🍯
Key point: The 100 inferences take ~10 minutes total. The thresholds are averaged to smooth noise. They're model-specific (Llama2-7B layer 6) and apply to the evaluated datasets (Wikitext2, PIQA, Hellaswag).

② Online quantization — processing one KV vector at token t

At layer 6, a new key vector has 4096 elements. Using the thresholds from Example 1, split into groups and quantize. Assume the vector has 150 outer, 3700 middle, 246 inner values. How many bits total?

  1. Outer group (150 values): Apply group-shift (subtract T_o_hi or T_o_lo) to narrow range, then quantize to 5 bits each. Storage: 4 bits in dense matrix + 8 bits in sparse COO = 12 bits/value × 150 = 1800 bits.
  2. Middle group (3700 values): Quantize to 4 bits each. Storage: 4 bits/value × 3700 = 14,800 bits (dense).
  3. Inner group (246 values): Quantize to 5 bits each. Storage: 4 bits in dense + 8 bits in sparse = 12 bits/value × 246 = 2952 bits.
  4. Total: 1800 + 14,800 + 2952 = 19,552 bits for 4096 values. Effective bitwidth: 19,552 / 4096 = 4.77 bits/value.
  5. Compare to FP16: 4096 × 16 = 65,536 bits. Compression: 65,536 / 19,552 = 3.35× (70% reduction).
💡
Why group-shift works: Without shift, the outer group spans wide ranges requiring many bits. Shift by T_o_hi/lo narrows the range before quantization. The shift itself is reversible (add back at dequantization), but the subsequent quantization is lossy.

③ Throughput calculation — batch=256, Llama2-13B, 1K:1K sequence

Oaken-LPDDR at 1.1 TB/s, reading 4.82-bit KV cache vs A100 at 2.0 TB/s reading 16-bit KV. Both process 256 requests × 1K tokens/request. What's the speedup?

  1. A100 bandwidth consumption: Each decode step reads 256 × 1K tokens × 16 bits/token = 4,096K bits = 512 KB per layer. For 40 layers (Llama2-13B), 512 KB × 40 = 20.48 MB per decode step.
  2. A100 time per decode step: 20.48 MB / 2.0 TB/s = 0.01 ms (ignoring compute, which is negligible for large batches).
  3. Oaken bandwidth consumption: 256 × 1K × 4.82 bits = 1,234K bits = 154.25 KB per layer. × 40 layers = 6.17 MB per decode step.
  4. Oaken time per decode step: 6.17 MB / 1.1 TB/s = 0.0056 ms.
  5. Speedup: 0.01 / 0.0056 = 1.78×. (Actual paper result: 1.58× due to compute overhead and partial batching.)
🍯
Insight: Oaken's effective bandwidth is 1.1 TB/s × (16 / 4.82) = 3.65 TB/s, exceeding A100's 2.0 TB/s. The LPDDR capacity (256 GB) also avoids capacity limits for large batches.

④ Accuracy computation — perplexity on Wikitext2 for Llama2-7B

Original FP16: perplexity = 5.47. Oaken (4.82 effective bits): perplexity = 5.53. What's the relative degradation?

  1. Absolute difference: 5.53 - 5.47 = 0.06.
  2. Relative degradation: (5.53 - 5.47) / 5.47 = 0.011 = 1.1%.
  3. Compare to baselines: QServe (4.25 effective bits): 5.67 perplexity, 3.7% degradation. KVQuant (4.81 bits): achieves lower accuracy loss than Oaken (paper states Oaken is 0.54% worse than KVQuant, implying KVQuant ~0.33% loss).
  4. KVQuant's edge: Uses online topK sorting → higher accuracy but 2× slower due to sorting overhead. Oaken trades 0.54% worse accuracy for no sorting cost.
💡
The tradeoff: Oaken's accuracy loss is small for most applications (e.g., PIQA accuracy: 79.05% → 78.29%, a 0.76% absolute drop). The throughput gain (1.58×) dominates the modest accuracy cost.

Problem 1 · easy
Why does Oaken profile offline on Wikitext2 but apply the thresholds to PIQA, Hellaswag, and the tested workloads? Isn't the distribution different?
Show answer
The paper's empirical finding (Fig. 6b): KV value ranges remain consistent across the datasets tested (Wikitext2, PIQA, Hellaswag) for a given model and layer. The thresholds are model-specific (Llama2-7B layer 6) but dataset-agnostic across the evaluated workloads. Profiling on Wikitext2 extracts thresholds that work on the tested datasets because the model's weights (not the data) primarily determine the KV distribution.Misconception: that quantization thresholds must be calibrated per-dataset. Oaken's offline-online hybrid works because KV distributions are consistent across the evaluated datasets for a given model.
Problem 2 · medium
A KV vector has 4096 elements: 200 outer, 3700 middle, 196 inner. Outer/inner stored as 12 bits/value (4 dense + 8 sparse), middle as 4 bits. What's the effective bitwidth? Why not just use 5 bits for everything?
Show answer
Total bits: 200×12 + 3700×4 + 196×12 = 2400 + 14,800 + 2352 = 19,552. Effective: 19,552/4096 = 4.77 bits/value. Using 5 bits for everything: 4096×5 = 20,480 bits (4.6% more). Oaken's split saves bits on the dominant middle group (90% at 4 bits) while handling outliers separately. Uniform 5-bit loses that advantage.Misconception: that uniform quantization is simpler. It is, but it wastes bits on inliers (4 bits suffice for 90%) and can't exploit the zeroed-slot fusion trick.
Problem 3 · medium
Oaken's group-shift subtracts T_o_hi from values > T_o_hi. A value x = 50, T_o_hi = 8. After shift: x' = 50 - 8 = 42. Quantize to 5 bits: Q(x') = round((42 - min) × σ). What's σ if min=0, max=50 after shift? Is this lossless?
Show answer
For 5-bit quantization, σ = (2^5 - 1) / (max - min) = 31 / 50 = 0.62. Then Q(42) = round((42 - 0) × 0.62) = round(26.04) = 26. At dequantization: x̂ = 26 / 0.62 + 0 + 8 (add back T_o_hi) = 42 + 8 = 50 in this example. The shift itself is reversible (add back T_o_hi), but the quantization step introduces rounding error, making the overall process lossy even though this specific example reconstructs exactly.Misconception: that the shift-and-quantize process is lossless. The shift is reversible, but uniform quantization is inherently lossy due to rounding. The worked example reconstructs exactly only due to chosen numbers.
Problem 4 · hard
Why does Oaken's fused dense-and-sparse encoding embed 4 bits of outliers in the dense matrix's zeroed slots, instead of storing all 5 bits in the sparse table? Construct a storage-cost comparison.
Show answer
Option 1 (all-sparse): 5 value + 6 index + 1 group = 12 bits/outlier. Option 2 (Oaken's fusion): 4 bits in dense (zeroed slot) + 8 bits in sparse (6 idx + 1 grp + 1 sign) = 12 bits total but memory-aligned (8-bit sparse entries). For 400 outliers: Option 1 = 400×12 = 4800 bits of sparse storage. Option 2 = 400×8 = 3200 bits sparse + 400×4 = 1600 bits dense (already allocated) = same 4800 bits total but sparse table is 33% smaller (3200 vs 4800), improving cache locality and burst efficiency. The fusion trades dense-matrix zeros (otherwise wasted) for sparse-table compactness.Misconception: that fusion is purely a space optimization. It also improves memory access patterns — smaller sparse tables mean fewer cache misses and better burst reads.
Problem 5 · medium
Oaken's MMU maintains two page tables (dense, sparse) in a single address space. Why not separate address spaces for dense and sparse, to simplify the design?
Show answer
Separate address spaces would require the DMA to switch between them when reading mixed dense/sparse pages for the same KV token, incurring address translation overhead and fragmenting burst reads. A single address space lets the MMU issue one sequential read spanning both dense and sparse pages, maximizing burst efficiency. The dual-table design is internal (virtual-to-physical mapping) but the physical layout is unified, so memory controllers see a single contiguous stream.Misconception: that dual tables = dual address spaces. Oaken's tables are logical (for different page sizes) but share a single physical address space — the MMU abstracts the complexity from the memory controller.
Problem 6 · medium
KVQuant uses online topK sorting to identify outliers per-token. Oaken uses offline thresholds. For a 4096-element vector, what's the time complexity of each? Why does Oaken's O(n) beat KVQuant's O(n log n) in practice?
Show answer
KVQuant: O(n log n) per token (topK sort). Oaken: O(n) per token (single scan to classify into three groups). For n=4096: O(n log n) = 4096 × log₂(4096) = 4096 × 12 = 49,152 ops. O(n) = 4096 ops. Oaken is 12× fewer operations. In hardware, sorting requires complex comparators and multi-cycle latency; Oaken's threshold comparisons are single-cycle. The paper reports KVQuant's overhead negates its accuracy gains — Oaken's O(n) hides latency via overlap with memory ops.Misconception: that O(n log n) is "good enough" for small n. For n=4096, 12× overhead every token across 256 batches × 1000 tokens = 3M tokens/second → 36M extra ops/second, which GPUs can't hide.
Problem 7 · hard
Oaken achieves 1.58× throughput over A100+QServe at batch=256. But at batch=16, A100 outperforms Oaken (Fig. 11a). Explain why, using the bandwidth-capacity tradeoff. Does Oaken always win at large batches?
Show answer
At batch=16, the KV cache is small. A100's 2.0 TB/s HBM is underutilized (compute-bound, not memory-bound). Oaken's compression doesn't help because bandwidth isn't the bottleneck — the A100's higher compute throughput dominates. At batch=256, larger KV cache creates memory pressure. Oaken's 256 GB LPDDR + 70% compression makes 1.1 TB/s act like 3.65 TB/s effective, exceeding A100's 2.0 TB/s. However, the crossover depends on model configuration: GQA, sliding-window attention, and MoE models reduce KV-cache pressure, changing the break-even point. Oaken doesn't universally win at all large batches—results are model- and workload-dependent.Misconception: that compression always helps and Oaken always wins at large batches. Compression only helps when memory is the bottleneck. The sweet spot varies by model architecture and memory configuration.
Problem 8 · easy
Oaken's quantization engine adds 1.86% area overhead, dequantization adds 6.35%. Why is dequantization 3.4× larger?
Show answer
Quantization (write path): single pass, decompose → shift → quantize → merge. Dequantization (read path): reverse all steps, including zero-insert (reconstructing sparse positions) and buffering to synchronize dense/sparse streams. The zero-insert shifter and dual buffers (inlier + outlier dequantizers) add logic. Also, dequantization is latency-critical (on the read path to processing units), so it's pipelined more aggressively, adding registers. Quantization can tolerate higher latency (overlapped with memory writes).Misconception: that quantization and dequantization are symmetric. Dequantization's zero-insert and synchronization add complexity beyond quantization's zero-remove.

Frequently asked questions

Twelve questions newcomers ask most. Tap any to expand.

Q1What does "online-offline hybrid" actually mean here?▾
Offline: profile ~100 inferences to extract four magnitude thresholds per layer (T_o_lo, T_i_lo, T_i_hi, T_o_hi). One-time, ~10 min. Online: use those thresholds to split each new KV vector into three groups (outer, middle, inner) at inference time, in O(n) per token. No online sorting or calibration — the thresholds are precomputed and reused.
Q2Why does offline profiling work across different datasets?▾
Empirical finding (Fig. 6b): KV value ranges are model-specific (vary by model & layer) but consistent across the datasets tested in the paper (Wikitext2, PIQA, Hellaswag). The model's weights (fixed) determine the distribution for the evaluated workloads. So thresholds profiled on Wikitext2 apply to the tested datasets.
Q3How does group-shift compress wide-range outliers into 5 bits?▾
Without shift, outer group spans wide ranges requiring many bits. Shift: subtract T_o_hi from values > T_o_hi (and T_o_lo from values < T_o_lo), narrowing the range before quantization. At dequantization, add back the threshold. The shift itself is reversible, but the subsequent uniform quantization introduces rounding errors, making the overall process lossy.
Q4What's the effective bitwidth, and why does it vary by model?▾
Effective bitwidth = (total bits for dense + sparse) / (total values). For Llama2-7B: 4.82 bits/value. For Llama2-70B: 4.89 bits. The paper uses a fixed group ratio (4/90/6) across all evaluations; variation comes from the specific threshold values and resulting quantization overhead per model.
Q5Why fuse outliers into the dense matrix instead of storing them separately?▾
Fusion trades zeroed dense slots (otherwise wasted) for sparse-table compactness. Without fusion: 5 value + 6 index + 1 group = 12 bits/outlier in sparse table. With fusion: 4 bits in dense (zeroed slot) + 8 bits in sparse (6 idx + 1 grp + 1 sign) = same 12 bits total but sparse table is 8-bit aligned (better for memory controllers). Also, smaller sparse table = better cache locality.
Q6How does the MMU handle variable-sized sparse pages?▾
Two tables: dense (predictable 4-bit pages) and sparse (variable 8-bit COO entries). Both map virtual addresses to physical pages in a single address space. On write, MMU allocates pages on-demand. On read, MMU issues burst reads for sequential tokens (dense pages laid out contiguously). The dual-table design avoids fragmentation while maximizing burst efficiency.
Q7Does Oaken always beat GPU baselines?▾
No. At small batches (≤16), A100's higher compute throughput dominates — memory isn't the bottleneck yet. Oaken wins at large batches where memory becomes the bottleneck and compression unlocks throughput. However, results are model- and configuration-dependent: GQA, sliding-window attention, and MoE models reduce KV-cache pressure, changing the crossover point. Oaken-HBM (80 GB) also has capacity limits vs Oaken-LPDDR (256 GB).
Q8Why does Oaken lose 0.87% accuracy vs FP16, while KVQuant loses less?▾
The paper states Oaken has 0.87% average loss vs FP16 and is 0.54% lower accuracy than KVQuant, implying KVQuant loses about 0.33% on average. KVQuant uses online topK sorting to isolate outliers more precisely, achieving higher accuracy. But the sorting overhead is 12× more operations than Oaken's O(n) threshold scan, negating the speedup. Oaken trades 0.54% worse accuracy for no sorting cost. For most applications, 0.87% loss is acceptable (e.g., PIQA: 79.05% → 78.29% = 0.76% absolute drop).
Q9What's the area overhead breakdown?▾
Table 4 reports quantization engine: 1.86% of compute core area, dequantization engine: 6.35%, totaling 8.21%. MMU area is reported separately. For context, the compute core (MPU + VPU) is 22.86% + 6.03% = 28.89% of total area. Oaken's quant+dequant engines are 8.21% of compute core area — significant but worthwhile for 1.58× throughput gain.
Q10Can Oaken integrate with any LLM accelerator, or is it LPU-specific?▾
Oaken's engines are designed for integration into any DMA unit (common in modern accelerators). The paper evaluates on LPU but notes the design can work with GPUs, NPUs, or custom LLM accelerators. The MMU requires page-based memory management, which most accelerators already have. The quantization/dequantization logic is standalone and portable.
Q11Why does Oaken use three groups instead of two or four?▾
Table 3 ablation: two groups (outer + middle) → 5.55 perplexity (vs 5.53 for three groups). Four groups → 5.52 perplexity (marginal 0.01 improvement) but effective bitwidth increases to 5.6 bits (vs 4.8 for three groups) due to extra group bits in sparse table. Three groups is the sweet spot: balances accuracy and bitwidth. The inner group (near-zero outliers) is necessary to prevent underflow during quantization.
Q12What did the authors leave for future work?▾
The paper doesn't explicitly list future work, but natural extensions: (1) dynamic threshold adjustment for fine-tuned models (currently requires re-profiling), (2) extension to 2-bit or 3-bit quantization for even higher compression, (3) integration with weight quantization (currently KV-only), (4) support for extremely long sequences (>32K tokens) where capacity pressure intensifies. The paper focuses on batched serving; single-request low-latency scenarios aren't addressed.

Core terms
OakenThe paper's acceleration solution: online-offline hybrid KV cache quantization + hardware modules (quant/dequant engines, MMU).
KV cacheCached key & value activations from past tokens in attention layers. Grows with sequence length × batch size.
Online-offline hybridOffline: profile to extract thresholds (one-time). Online: apply thresholds per-token (fast, O(n)).
Three groupsOuter (4%, large outliers), middle (90%, inliers), inner (6%, near-zero outliers). Defined by four thresholds.
Group-shiftSubtract threshold before quantizing to narrow the range of wide-magnitude groups. Reversible transformation, but overall process (shift + quantization) is lossy.
Fused dense-and-sparseStore inliers densely (4 bits), embed 4 bits of outliers in zeroed dense slots, store rest (6 idx + 1 grp + 1 sign) in sparse COO table.
Effective bitwidthAverage bits per value after quantization + sparse overhead. Oaken: 4.82 bits (Llama2-7B) vs 16 FP16 = 70% reduction.
Quantization/dequantization enginesOaken's engines in the DMA unit that quantize on write and dequantize on read. Table 4: quant 1.86%, dequant 6.35% of compute-core area.
Supporting terms
HBM / LPDDRHigh-Bandwidth Memory (2 TB/s, 80 GB per GPU; paper uses 2×A100 = 160 GB) vs Low-Power DDR (1.1 TB/s, 256 GB). Bandwidth-capacity tradeoff.
Batch sizeNumber of concurrent LLM requests processed together. Larger batches → more KV cache → memory pressure.
Attention layerTransformer component where Q × K^T and score × V occur. KV cache enables reuse across decode steps.
Prefill vs generationPrefill: process input tokens (compute-bound). Generation: autoregressively decode output tokens (memory-bound).
Outlier / inlierValues with extreme magnitude (outer/inner groups) vs typical magnitude (middle group).
Quantization scale (σ)σ = (2^m - 1) / (max - min), where m is target bitwidth. Computed online per-token per-group.
COO formatCoordinate List sparse matrix format: (index, value) pairs. Oaken uses 6 idx + 1 grp + 1 sign = 8 bits/entry.
Zero-remove / zero-insert shifterHardware modules that compact sparse matrices (remove zeros on write, insert zeros on read).
MMU (Memory Management Unit)Handles dense/sparse page tables, virtual-to-physical address mapping, burst reads. Single address space.
DMA unitDirect Memory Access unit in accelerators. Oaken integrates quant/dequant engines here.
PerplexityLanguage model evaluation metric. Lower is better. Oaken: 5.53 vs FP16: 5.47 on Llama2-7B Wikitext2.
MPKI (Misses Per 1000 Instructions)Not directly used in Oaken paper, but relevant for memory-bound analysis.

Related papers

KVQuant: Towards 10M Context LLM Inference hard

Hooper et al. · arXiv 2024

The topK-sorting baseline Oaken improves on. Achieves lower accuracy loss (~0.33% vs Oaken's 0.87%) but at 2× latency cost due to sorting overhead. Oaken's key innovation is avoiding this cost via offline profiling.

QServe: W4A8KV4 Quantization and System Co-design medium

Lin et al. · arXiv 2024

Channel-reordering baseline using SmoothQuant transformation. Faster than KVQuant but loses more accuracy than Oaken. Shows the fast-but-less-accurate end of the tradeoff space.

Tender: Tensor Decomposition and Runtime Requantization medium

Lee et al. · ISCA 2024

Systolic-array accelerator with coarse quantization. Loses significant accuracy (NaN on Mixtral-8x7B) due to low granularity. Oaken's per-vector grouping avoids this.

Atom: Low-bit Quantization for Efficient LLM Serving hard

Zhao et al. · arXiv 2024

Channel grouping + indirect indexing. Effective bitwidth 4.25 but accuracy loss higher than Oaken. The paper notes runtime overhead of reordering.

FlashAttention: Fast and Memory-Efficient Exact Attention hard

Dao et al. · NeurIPS 2022

IO-aware attention that reduces memory access via tiling. Orthogonal to Oaken — could combine FlashAttention's tiling with Oaken's KV quantization for further gains.

Paper facts (directly from "Oaken: Fast and Efficient LLM Serving with Online-Offline Hybrid KV Cache Quantization," Kim et al., ISCA '25):

  • Throughput gains: up to 1.58× over A100+QServe, 1.79× over A100+vLLM (Fig. 11, batch=256, Llama2-13B).
  • Bitwidth reduction: effective 4.82 bits (Llama2-7B), 4.89 bits (Llama2-70B) vs 16 FP16 = up to 70% reduction (Table 2).
  • Accuracy loss: avg 0.87% across 8 LLMs (Wikitext2 perplexity, PIQA/Winogrande/Hellaswag accuracy), paper states Oaken is 0.54% lower accuracy than KVQuant (Table 2).
  • Offline profiling: ~100 inferences, ~10 min for Llama2-70B (Section 6.1).
  • Group ratios: 4% outer, 90% middle, 6% inner (consistent across all evaluations, Section 6.1).
  • Hardware overhead: Table 4 reports quantization engine 1.86%, dequantization engine 6.35% of compute core area, totaling 8.21%. MMU area reported separately.
